# Introduction

Bathymetric Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) has significantly revolutionized advanced surveying in underwater construction and coastal projects. Unlike conventional sonar technologies, Bathymetric LiDAR allows you to acquire detailed and accurate topographical data from submerged areas. This tutorial will introduce you to the basic functioning of this tool, its applications, and the potential challenges that may be encountered during its use.

## Step-by-Step Guide

### Understanding the Basics

1. **Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR)**: LIDAR is a remote sensing method that employs light in the form of pulsed laser to measure varying distances to the Earth. 
2. **Bathymetric LIDAR**: It is a type of LIDAR that's designed specifically for penetrating the water surface to measure depths. It's widely used in coastal engineering and underwater construction to map the seafloor.

### Equipment and Software

Bathymetric LiDAR system requires three essential components:

- An **aircraft** for the transportation of the system
- A **LIDAR unit**, which includes a GPS for positional data, a submarine laser system for depth measurements, and an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) for correcting positional and altitude errors.
- A **software system** for processing and analysing acquired data

Popular software solutions include `QPS Qimera` and `Leica Infinity`. These systems help remove noise, process data, and create 3D models of the surveyed area.

### Deployment Procedure

```markdown
Step 1: Pre-survey planning
```
During this phase, plan your flight paths, known as flight lines, over the survey area. Also, check environmental conditions as they can influence data quality.

```markdown
Step 2: Data Acquisition 
```
Fly your LIDAR-equipped aircraft at a planned altitude above the waterbody. The LIDAR system sends pulses of laser light towards the water surface and measures time taken for reflected light to return.

```markdown
Step 3: Data Processing
```
Extract the relevant data from raw data. The software will filter out irrelevant reflections (called 'returns'), like those from fish or water surface, and retain only those from seabed.

```markdown
Step 4: Depth Calculation
```
By knowing the speed of light in water and the time for the light to travel to the sea floor and back, the depth of the ocean can be calculated. 

```markdown
Step 5: Creation of Bathymetric Map 
```
The processed and corrected data are then collated to create a detailed bathymetric map or a 3D model.

### Potential Challenges

1. **Turbidity**: High turbidity, caused by particles in water, can impact the laser’s ability to penetrate the waterbody effectively.
2. **Water Reflectance**: In cases where the water surface is rough or reflective, the signals might get distorted or deflected.
3. **Biological Distortions**: Presence of marine lifeforms in the path of the laser can lead to erroneous readings.

## Conclusion

Bathymetric LiDAR has robust potential in surveying underwater landscapes for construction or environmental projects. It brings accuracy, efficiency and safety. However, like any technology, it requires skilled handling and the right processing tools to deliver the best outcomes. Understanding the tool’s strengths and weaknesses can help you harness its potential successfully.
