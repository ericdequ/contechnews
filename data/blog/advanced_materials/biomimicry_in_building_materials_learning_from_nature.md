# Biomimicry in Building Materials: Learning from Natures Designs

Biomimicry, as a field of study and innovation, unites biology and technology, strolling the vistas where these domains intersect. It proposes the notion that nature, an excellent, time-tested engineer itself, holds the key to some of our most complex problems. Within the construction industry, it births materials that mirror the genius supplied by millions of years of natural evolution.

## Understanding Biomimicry 

Biomimicry, or biomimetics, involves the simulation of elements of nature for the purpose of solving intricate human challenges. The basic idea is that nature has already resolved many of the challenges we're grappling with: energy, climate control, non-toxic chemistry, transportation, and others. Well-known examples include the bullet train in Japan, inspired by the kingfishers’ beak, and Velcro, which drew its design from the mechanism of a burdock burr attaching to fur.

## Role of Biomimicry in Construction

Biomimicry carries immense potential for the construction sector. Not only can these nature-inspired designs introduce substantial cost savings and energy efficiency, but they can also contribute to creating sustainable, eco-friendly buildings. Below are some remarkable developments in biomimicry-enabled building materials:

### 1. BioMASON Bricks

Hinging on the concept of biocementation, BioMASON bricks are grown rather than baked. Tiny microorganisms produce a binding agent that leads to the formation of these bricks, similar to coral reefs’ formation. This approach eradicates the need for high-temperature kilns, resulting in substantial energy savings.

### 2. Self-healing Concrete

Taking notes from human skin's ability to heal wounds, scientists developed concrete with self-healing abilities. If damaged, a self-regenerating bacterial agent within the concrete triggers the filling of cracks, prolonging the material's life and reducing maintenance requirements.

### 3. Transparent Wood

Drawing inspiration from the clear undersea structures of mantis shrimp, Swedish researchers have developed transparent wood. This material allows natural light to pass through, thus reducing dependence on artificial lighting.
